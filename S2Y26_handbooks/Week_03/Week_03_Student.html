“The Fishery Manager’s Dilemma”

SCENARIO

You are a scientific advisor to the Western Australian Fisheries Authority. The authority manages a sardine fishery that has shown signs of overfishing in recent years.

Marine biologists have developed a Schaefer model for the sardine population, using parameters \(g = 0.4\) per year and \(K = 1500\) tonnes.

Current situation: - Stock level: \(S = 1200\) tonnes - Industry harvest: \(H = 120\) tonnes/year

The Fisheries Authority wants to know: Is this harvest sustainable?

YOUR TASK

Part A: Understanding the Schaefer Model (20 minutes)

  1. Calculate the growth rate \(G(S)\) at three stock levels:
    • \(S = 400\) tonnes (heavily depleted)
    • \(S = 750\) tonnes (half of carrying capacity)
    • \(S = 1200\) tonnes (current level)
  2. Show that growth is maximized when \(S = K/2 = 750\) tonnes:
    • Expand the Schaefer equation: \(G(S) = gS - \frac{g}{K}S^2\)
    • This is a quadratic with vertex at \(S = \frac{K}{2}\)
    • Calculate the Maximum Sustainable Yield (MSY)
  3. Plot \(G(S)\) vs. \(S\) for \(S\) ranging from 0 to 1500 tonnes.

Part B: Sustainability Analysis (20 minutes)

  1. A harvest is sustainable if the population can grow enough to replace what’s removed: \[G(S) \geq H\]

    At the current stock level (\(S = 1200\) tonnes):

    • Calculate \(G(1200)\)
    • Is the current harvest of 120 tonnes/year sustainable?

  2. Find the equilibrium stock levels where \(G(S) = H\):

    • Set up: \(0.4S(1 - S/1500) = 120\)
    • Expand: \(0.4S - 0.0002667S^2 = 120\)
    • Rearrange to standard form: \(0.0002667S^2 - 0.4S + 120 = 0\)
    • Solve using the quadratic formula
    • There should be two solutions—what do they represent?

  3. The lower equilibrium is unstable (if stock drops below it, population collapses). The upper equilibrium is stable (population returns to it if perturbed).

    • Which equilibrium is safer for the fishery?
    • Is the current stock (\(S = 1200\)) above or below the safe equilibrium?

Part C: Policy Recommendation (15 minutes)

  1. The fishing industry wants to increase harvest to 140 tonnes/year due to high demand.
    • Find the new equilibrium stock levels for \(H = 140\)
    • What is the minimum stock level needed to sustain this harvest?
    • Compare to the \(H = 120\) scenario—is the risk acceptable?
  2. Write a brief recommendation (4–5 sentences) to the Fisheries Authority:
    • Is the current 120 tonne/year harvest sustainable?
    • Should the 140 tonne/year increase be approved?
    • What harvest level would you recommend for long-term sustainability?

CONCEPT REMINDERS

Use the notes below if your group needs a quick reminder of how to read the model while working through the questions.

The Schaefer (1957) model focuses directly on the growth function \(G(S)\) rather than the population trajectory \(P(t)\). This is particularly useful for fisheries management because managers need to compare annual growth with annual harvest.

\[G(S) = g \cdot S \cdot \left(1 - \frac{S}{K}\right)\]

where: - \(G(S)\) = growth (flow) of fish biomass - \(g\) = intrinsic growth rate - \(S\) = current stock level - \(K\) = carrying capacity

The term \(\left(1 - \frac{S}{K}\right)\) is the compensating factor:

Stock Level Compensating Factor Effect
\(S \ll K\) \((1 - S/K) \approx 1\) Growth near intrinsic rate
\(S = K/2\) \((1 - S/K) = 0.5\) Maximum total growth
\(S = K\) \((1 - S/K) = 0\) Zero growth (at capacity)
\(S > K\) \((1 - S/K) < 0\) Negative growth (decline)

Because \(G(S)\) is a downward-opening quadratic, the Maximum Sustainable Yield (MSY) occurs at the vertex:

\[S_{MSY} = \frac{K}{2}\] \[G_{MSY} = \frac{gK}{4}\]

A harvest is sustainable when the population can grow fast enough to replace what is removed:

\[G(S) \geq H\]

At equilibrium, harvest exactly matches growth:

\[G(S) = H\]

For a Schaefer fishery, this equation often has two equilibrium stock levels:

  • a lower equilibrium, which is unstable because any further drop can trigger collapse
  • an upper equilibrium, which is stable because the stock tends to recover toward it after small disturbances

This is why fisheries policy is not only about whether a harvest is mathematically possible. It is also about how much safety margin remains above the unstable threshold.

HINTS / SCAFFOLDING

Quadratic formula: \[S = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}\]

For the equation \(0.0002667S^2 - 0.4S + 120 = 0\): - \(a = 0.0002667\) - \(b = -0.4\) - \(c = 120\)

Python template:

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

S = np.linspace(0, 1500, 500)
G = 0.4 * S * (1 - S/1500)

plt.figure(figsize=(10, 6))
plt.plot(S, G, linewidth=2, label='Growth G(S)')
plt.axhline(y=120, color='r', linestyle='--', label='Harvest = 120 tonnes/year')
plt.axhline(y=140, color='orange', linestyle='--', label='Proposed = 140 tonnes/year')
plt.xlabel('Stock Level (tonnes)')
plt.ylabel('Growth Rate (tonnes/year)')
plt.title('Sardine Fishery: Schaefer Model')
plt.legend()
plt.grid(True, alpha=0.3)
plt.show()

“Abalone Fishery: Sustainable Harvest in Western Australia”

SCENARIO

The WA Department of Fisheries manages a commercial abalone (Haliotis roei) fishery along the south coast. Population surveys and growth studies have led to the following Schaefer model:

$G(S) = 0.35 S (1 - )$

where:

  • $G(S)$ = annual population growth (tonnes/year)
  • $S$ = current stock level (tonnes)
  • Intrinsic growth rate $g = 0.35$
  • Carrying capacity $K = 1200$ tonnes

Current situation: Stock level is $S = 950$ tonnes. Industry wants to harvest 100 tonnes/year.

YOUR TASK

Part A: Understanding the Schaefer Model (15 minutes)

  1. Calculate growth $G(S)$ at three stock levels:

    • $S = 300$ tonnes (low stock)
    • $S = 600$ tonnes (mid stock)
    • $S = 900$ tonnes (high stock)

  2. Show that growth is maximized at $S = K/2 = 600$ tonnes

Part B: Sustainability Analysis (20 minutes)

  1. A harvest is sustainable if $G(S) H$ (growth ≥ harvest).

    • At current stock $S = 950$ tonnes, what is $G(950)$ ?
    • Is the proposed harvest of 100 tonnes/year sustainable?

  2. Find the two equilibrium points where $G(S) = 100$ :

    • Solve: $0.35S(1 - S/1200) = 100$
    • Hint: This is a quadratic equation. Use the quadratic formula.
    • Interpret: what do these two equilibrium points represent?

  3. The lower equilibrium is unstable (population collapses if stock drops below it). The upper equilibrium is stable (population recovers to it).

    • Which equilibrium is safer for long-term sustainability?

Part C: Policy Recommendation (15 minutes)

  1. The industry proposes increasing harvest to 120 tonnes/year due to high market prices.

    • Find the new equilibrium points for $H = 120$
    • What is the minimum safe stock level?
    • Compare to the $H = 100$ scenario

  2. Write a 4-5 sentence recommendation to the Fisheries Minister addressing:

    • Whether the 120 tonne harvest is sustainable

    • An alternative harvest level you would recommend

  1. Create a graph showing:
    • The growth curve $G(S)$ vs stock $S$

PYTHON TEMPLATE

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

S = np.linspace(0, 1200, 500)
G = 0.35 * S * (1 - S/1200)

plt.figure(figsize=(10, 6))
plt.plot(S, G, linewidth=2, label='Growth G(S)', color='blue')
plt.axhline(y=100, color='orange', linestyle='--', label='H = 100 tonnes/year')
plt.axhline(y=120, color='red', linestyle='--', label='H = 120 tonnes/year')
plt.xlabel('Stock Level S (tonnes)')
plt.ylabel('Growth Rate G(S) (tonnes/year)')
plt.title('Abalone Fishery: Schaefer Growth Model')
plt.legend()
plt.grid(True, alpha=0.3)
plt.xlim(0, 1200)
plt.ylim(0, 120)

# Mark MSY point
S_msy = 600
G_msy = 0.35 * S_msy * (1 - S_msy/1200)
plt.plot(S_msy, G_msy, 'go', markersize=10, label=f'MSY = {G_msy:.1f} tonnes/year')

plt.legend()
plt.show()

“Rock Lobster Recovery: From Overfishing to Sustainability”

SCENARIO

Western Australia’s rock lobster (Panulirus cygnus) fishery was severely overfished in the 1990s. After implementing catch limits, the fishery is recovering. Marine biologists have developed this Schaefer model:

$G(S) = 0.42 S (1 - )$

where:

  • $G(S)$ = annual population growth (tonnes/year)
  • $S$ = current stock level (tonnes)
  • Intrinsic growth rate $g = 0.42$ (higher than abalone due to faster maturation)
  • Carrying capacity $K = 800$ tonnes

Current situation: After recovery efforts, stock is now $S = 350$ tonnes. The fishing industry wants permission to harvest 70 tonnes/year.

YOUR TASK

Part A: Schaefer Model Analysis (15 minutes)

  1. Calculate growth $G(S)$ at three stock levels:

    • $S = 200$ tonnes (recovering from collapse)
    • $S = 400$ tonnes (near optimal)
    • $S = 700$ tonnes (near carrying capacity)
    • At which stock does growth peak?

  2. Prove that maximum growth occurs at $S^* = K/2 = 400$ tonnes

    • Write $G(S) = 0.42S - 0.000525S^2$ in standard form
    • Complete the square or use vertex formula
    • Calculate the maximum sustainable yield (MSY)

Part B: Harvest Sustainability (20 minutes)

  1. At the current stock level $S = 350$ tonnes:

    • Calculate the natural growth rate $G(350)$
    • Is the proposed 70 tonnes/year harvest sustainable?
    • What fraction of growth would be harvested?

  2. Find all stock levels where harvest equals growth: $G(S) = 70$

    • Set up: $0.42S(1 - S/800) = 70$
    • Multiply out: $0.42S - 0.000525S^2 = 70$
    • Rearrange: $0.000525S^2 - 0.42S + 70 = 0$
    • Solve using quadratic formula
    • Interpret: why are there two solutions?

  3. Stability analysis:

    • The lower equilibrium represents a “danger zone”
    • The upper equilibrium is the target sustainable level
    • Which equilibrium is closer to current stock?

Part C: Climate Change Impact (15 minutes)

  1. Ocean warming is expected to reduce carrying capacity to $K = 720$ tonnes by 2040.

    • What is the new MSY under warming scenario?
    • New model: $G(S) = 0.42S(1 - S/720)$
    • Can the fishery still sustain 70 tonnes/year harvest?

  2. Write a brief (4-5 sentence) climate adaptation strategy for the fishery addressing:

    • Short-term harvest levels while stock rebuilds
    • Long-term harvest levels accounting for warming
    • Monitoring requirements

Part D: Comparative Analysis (10 minutes)

  1. Compare rock lobster to abalone (from Problem W3A):
