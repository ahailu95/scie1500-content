“Optimizing Chemical Reactions in Pharmaceutical Manufacturing”

SCENARIO

A pharmaceutical company is optimizing the production of a critical antibiotic. The yield of the drug depends on reaction temperature. Chemical engineers have developed an empirical model:

\[Y(T) = -0.05T^2 + 8T - 200\]

where: - \(Y(T)\) = yield (grams of product per batch) - \(T\) = reaction temperature (°C) - Valid range: \(50°C \leq T \leq 110°C\)

The company wants to maximize yield while staying within safe operating temperatures.

YOUR TASK

Part A: Understanding Rates of Change (20 minutes)

  1. Calculate the yield at three temperatures:

    • \(T = 60°C\)
    • \(T = 80°C\)
    • \(T = 100°C\)

    Which temperature gives the highest yield?

  2. Find the derivative \(Y'(T) = \frac{dY}{dT}\):

    • Use the power rule: \((x^n)' = nx^{n-1}\)
    • Interpret: What does \(Y'(T)\) represent? Include units!

  3. Calculate \(Y'(70)\) and \(Y'(90)\):

    • What does the sign tell you about whether yield is increasing or decreasing?
    • Express each as a rate per degree Celsius

Part B: Finding the Optimal Temperature (20 minutes)

  1. Find the critical point by solving \(Y'(T) = 0\):
    • Set your derivative equal to zero
    • Solve for \(T\)
    • This is the candidate for maximum yield
  2. Verify this is a maximum using the second derivative test:
    • Calculate \(Y''(T)\)
    • Evaluate \(Y''(T_{critical})\)
    • If \(Y'' < 0\): maximum ✓
    • If \(Y'' > 0\): minimum ✗
  3. Is the optimal temperature within the safe range \([50, 110]°C\)?
    • If YES: use it
    • If NO: check the boundary values

Part C: Practical Constraints (15 minutes)

  1. The reaction vessel can only maintain temperature within ±3°C of the setpoint.
    • If the optimal temperature is \(T_{opt}\), the actual temperature varies in \([T_{opt} - 3, T_{opt} + 3]\)
    • Calculate the yield at both extremes
    • What’s the range of possible yields?
  2. A competing process uses \(T = 70°C\) and claims “good enough” yields.
    • How much yield is lost compared to the optimum?
    • Express as a percentage

Part D: Cost-Benefit Analysis (5 minutes)

  1. Higher temperatures require more energy. Energy cost is \(\$0.50\) per degree above 50°C per batch.
    • Calculate the energy cost at the optimal temperature
    • If the drug sells for \(\$5\) per gram, calculate net profit per batch at optimal temperature
    • Compare to net profit at \(T = 70°C\)

CONCEPT REMINDERS

Use the notes below if your group needs a quick reminder of how to interpret the derivative while solving the task.

  • If \(Y'(T) > 0\), yield is increasing as temperature increases.
  • If \(Y'(T) < 0\), yield is decreasing as temperature increases.
  • A critical point occurs where \(Y'(T) = 0\).
  • If \(Y''(T) < 0\), the curve is concave down there, so the critical point is a local maximum.
  • If the mathematical optimum lies outside the safe range, compare the boundary values instead.

HINTS / SCAFFOLDING

Power rule: - \((x^n)' = nx^{n-1}\) - \((c \cdot f(x))' = c \cdot f'(x)\) where \(c\) is a constant - \((f(x) + g(x))' = f'(x) + g'(x)\)

For \(Y(T) = -0.05T^2 + 8T - 200\): - \(Y'(T) = -0.1T + 8\) - \(Y''(T) = -0.1\)

Python template:

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

T = np.linspace(50, 110, 500)
Y = -0.05 * T**2 + 8 * T - 200

# Derivative
dY_dT = -0.1 * T + 8

# Critical point
T_crit = 80  # From Y'(T) = 0
Y_crit = -0.05 * T_crit**2 + 8 * T_crit - 200

plt.figure(figsize=(10, 6))
plt.plot(T, Y, linewidth=2, label='Yield Y(T)')
plt.plot(T_crit, Y_crit, 'ro', markersize=10, label=f'Optimum: T={T_crit}°C, Y={Y_crit:.1f}g')
plt.axvline(x=50, color='gray', linestyle='--', alpha=0.5)
plt.axvline(x=110, color='gray', linestyle='--', alpha=0.5)
plt.xlabel('Temperature (°C)')
plt.ylabel('Yield (grams/batch)')
plt.title('Drug Yield vs Reaction Temperature')
plt.legend()
plt.grid(True, alpha=0.3)
plt.show()

“Drug Pharmacokinetics: Dosing for Diabetes Management”

SCENARIO

You are a pharmacologist designing a dosing protocol for metformin, a diabetes medication. After oral administration, blood concentration follows:

$C(t) = 85(e^{-0.12t} - e^{-0.45t})$

where:

  • $C(t)$ = blood concentration (μg/mL)
  • $t$ = hours after ingestion
  • The double exponential accounts for absorption (fast) and elimination (slow)

Therapeutic constraints:

  • Minimum effective concentration: 15 μg/mL (below this, poor glucose control)
  • Maximum safe concentration: 100 μg/mL (above this, risk of lactic acidosis)
  • Patients must maintain therapeutic levels for 12-hour intervals between doses

YOUR TASK

Part A: Concentration Profile Analysis (20 minutes)

  1. Calculate concentration at $t = 0, 1, 2, 4, 8, 12$ hours

    • When does peak concentration occur? (look for the maximum in your table)
    • What is the peak value?

  2. Find the exact time of peak concentration using calculus:

    • Take the derivative: $C’(t) = 85[-0.12e^{-0.12t} - (-0.45)e^{-0.45t}]$
    • Set $C’(t) = 0$ and solve for $t$
    • Hint: $-0.12e^{-0.12t} + 0.45e^{-0.45t} = 0$
    • Rearrange: $0.45e^{-0.45t} = 0.12e^{-0.12t}$
    • Divide both sides by $e^{-0.45t}$ : $0.45 = 0.12e^{0.33t}$
    • Solve for $t$

  3. Verify that the peak is safe:

    • Calculate $C(t_{peak})$
    • Is it below 100 μg/mL?

Part B: Therapeutic Window (20 minutes)

  1. Determine when concentration drops below therapeutic threshold:

    • Solve numerically or graphically: $85(e^{-0.12t} - e^{-0.45t}) = 15$
    • Use Python to find the intersection point
    • How many hours of therapeutic coverage per dose?

  2. Can patients safely use a 12-hour dosing interval?

    • Compare therapeutic duration to 12 hours
    • If not sufficient, what minimum concentration will they have at 12 hours?

  3. To achieve 12-hour coverage with $C(12) = 15$ μg/mL, what dose multiplier is needed?

    • New model: $C(t) = D (e^{-0.12t} - e^{-0.45t})$ where $D$ is the dose factor
    • Solve: $D(e^{-0.12 } - e^{-0.45 }) = 15$
    • Does this keep peak concentration safe?

Part C: Multiple Dosing Strategy (15 minutes)

  1. If a second dose is taken at $t = 12$ hours:

    • Residual from first dose: $C_1(12) = 85(e^{-0.12 } - e^{-0.45 })$
    • New dose contribution: $C_2(t) = 85(e^{-0.12(t-12)} - e^{-0.45(t-12)})$ for $t $
    • Total concentration at $t = 12^+$ : $C_{total}(12) = C_1(12) + 85$
    • Will this cause unsafe accumulation?

  2. Steady state: After many doses, concentration oscillates between a minimum and maximum.

    • Estimate the steady-state maximum
    • Is it safe?

Part D: Rate of Elimination (10 minutes)

  1. Calculate the rate of change of concentration:

    • $C’(t) = 85[-0.12e^{-0.12t} + 0.45e^{-0.45t}]$
    • Evaluate at $t = 2$ hours (around peak)
    • Evaluate at $t = 10$ hours (late elimination phase)
    • Which elimination rate is faster? Why does this make sense physiologically?

  2. The two exponential terms represent:

    • $e^{-0.45t}$ : rapid absorption and distribution
    • $e^{-0.12t}$ : slower elimination
    • At what time does elimination become the dominant process?

PYTHON TEMPLATE

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from scipy.optimize import fsolve

t = np.linspace(0, 24, 500)
C = 85 * (np.exp(-0.12*t) - np.exp(-0.45*t))

# Derivative
dC_dt = 85 * (-0.12*np.exp(-0.12*t) + 0.45*np.exp(-0.45*t))

# Find peak time
def derivative(t):
    return 85 * (-0.12*np.exp(-0.12*t) + 0.45*np.exp(-0.45*t))

t_peak = fsolve(derivative, 2)[0]
C_peak = 85 * (np.exp(-0.12*t_peak) - np.exp(-0.45*t_peak))

fig, (ax1, ax2) = plt.subplots(2, 1, figsize=(10, 10))

# Top: Concentration
ax1.plot(t, C, linewidth=2, label='Blood Concentration C(t)')
ax1.axhline(y=15, color='r', linestyle='--', label='Minimum therapeutic (15 μg/mL)')
ax1.axhline(y=100, color='orange', linestyle='--', label='Maximum safe (100 μg/mL)')
ax1.plot(t_peak, C_peak, 'go', markersize=10, label=f'Peak at t={t_peak:.2f}h, C={C_peak:.1f} μg/mL')
ax1.fill_between(t, 15, 100, alpha=0.2, color='green', label='Therapeutic window')
ax1.set_xlabel('Time (hours)')
ax1.set_ylabel('Concentration (μg/mL)')
ax1.set_title('Metformin Pharmacokinetics')
ax1.legend()
ax1.grid(True, alpha=0.3)

# Bottom: Rate of change
ax2.plot(t, dC_dt, linewidth=2, color='purple', label="C'(t) = dC/dt")
ax2.axhline(y=0, color='k', linestyle='-', linewidth=0.5)
ax2.axvline(x=t_peak, color='g', linestyle=':', alpha=0.5, label=f'Peak at t={t_peak:.2f}h')
ax2.set_xlabel('Time (hours)')
ax2.set_ylabel('Rate of change (μg/mL/hour)')
ax2.set_title('Rate of Concentration Change')
ax2.legend()
ax2.grid(True, alpha=0.3)

plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()
